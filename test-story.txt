The lighthouse keeper had watched the same stretch of coast for thirty years. He knew the moods of the water the way a farmer knows his fields — the flat grey calm before a storm, the restless chop of an incoming tide, the strange stillness that meant fog was on its way. On the night the ship came in blind, he was already awake, already climbing the spiral stairs with his lamp, because the gulls had gone quiet an hour before and that silence had never once been wrong. He turned the great light to face the rocks, and he waited, and somewhere out in the dark a captain saw the beam and turned his wheel, and lived.
